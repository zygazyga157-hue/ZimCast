The most iconic football match between Zimbabwe and South Africa is the 1994 AFCON Qualifier on 16 August 1992, widely remembered as the birth of Zimbabwe’s "Dream Team." Zimbabwe defeated South Africa 4–1 at the National Sports Stadium in Harare, an upset that defined a generation for the Warriors. [1, 2, 3, 4] 
## Match Details & Players
The "Dream Team," led by German coach Reinhard Fabisch, dominated a South African side making their return to international football. [1, 3, 5]

## The Intense Five Minutes: The "Sunkuzonke" Solo (85' – 90')
While the whole match was electric, the final five minutes are legendary for Peter Ndlovu’s (No. 9) masterclass, which completely demoralised the South African defence.

* 85th Minute: After goals from Vitalis Takawira (No. 11) and Rahman Gumbo (No. 7) had already put Zimbabwe ahead, the atmosphere was at a fever pitch. South Africa tried to push forward for a consolation, but Zimbabwe's captain Francis Shonhayi (No. 4) intercepted the ball and launched a counter-attack.
* 87th Minute: Willard Khumalo (No. 8) found Peter Ndlovu (No. 9) on the wing. Ndlovu, nicknamed "Sunkuzonke," took on two defenders, showing the pace and dribbling that would soon make him a star in the English Premier League.
* 88th Minute (The Moment): Ndlovu (No. 9) cut inside the box, weaving past Lucas Radebe (No. 2) and Neil Tovey (No. 3) with blistering close control. He fired a low shot past Mark Anderson (No. 1) to make it 4–1.
* 90th Minute: The stadium erupted into "Nyama Yekugocha" (meat for roasting) chants. As the whistle blew, the fans stormed the pitch to celebrate a win that remains the gold standard for the Warriors. [1, 2, 3, 6, 9] 

## Tragic Mention: 9 July 2000 Qualifier [3] 
Another "iconic" match for darker reasons is the 2002 World Cup Qualifier at the same stadium. In the 83rd minute, after Delron Buckley scored to make it 2–0 for South Africa, fans threw bottles, leading police to fire tear gas. The resulting stampede tragically killed 12 people. [10, 11, 12]
